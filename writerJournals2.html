Writer’s Journal #2

Learning to Write

 

One learns to write through a systematic process of increasingly difficult challenges and goals, just like anything else.  When learning to talk, we have the challenge of communicating our wants more effectively to those who can fulfill them. We receive help and guidance through this and that is part of the process too. I use this as an example because I feel that this is the basis of human communication and writing stems from the foundation laid here. Once we start learning written language, the same process happens. Starting with the alphabet then we break down words into letters to further understand the most basic organization of writing. After that we form combinations with the words, eventually ending up with complete sentences. After that we focus on grammar, then branching out into different genres of writing. All the while given goals, setting goals, overcoming challenges and  much of the time having guidance of parents, teachers and peers. This learning is deepened and built upon through the practice, repetition, guidance and (what I find most important) applying that knowledge for oneself.

 

The understanding of the learning process I have comes from my own analysis and experiences. I think most would agree on the process, but we may not agree on which aspects matter thee most. Where I think applying what I’ve learned is the most important part of the process, a peer may find repetition tobe the most important tool in the process. Where I see a want to reach a goal or overcome a challenge ingrained into our purpose as the origin of my understanding; a peer may say that it stem from someone teaching us these things. We all learn differently and perceive the world from our own unique perspectives. So when I begin to think about views others may have, I conclude that w are both probably right to an extent and that chances are - alone, we only have part of the picture.
