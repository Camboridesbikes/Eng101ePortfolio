Writer’s Journal #3

Reflections

 

1.Reflecting on completed works can give us valuable perspectives - and  even insight into our own perspectives. It gives us a chance to look at our own thinking and presentation that helps us question how effective our ideas may have gotten across or even how clearly developed those ideas were. It can help one define a strategy or audience or what needs to be improved. Where more/different elaboration is needed,maybe even remove entire paragraphs as Sandra Giles spoke of one of her students doing.



This can help with understanding rhetorical knowledge and one’s own processes by putting one in a space to question the piece. I would imagine reflecting with the intent of viewing the writing from a position of objectivity. Detaching from the fact that you were trying to say something or even that you did say something. Then you can see what rhetorical habits you may be writing with and ask if they are effective or not.

 

  1. I view reflective writing as different from other types of writing in that it is tailored less for the reader gain or learn from, and more for the writer to learn from by nature. It is similar to note taking, just with maybe more structure and continuity? In the article we read, Sandra Giles talks about it being useful for the instructors, but even that is the most beneficial to the writer’s self-improvement interests.

 

3.Through the course I can reflect on my writing both as I write and after I complete assignments. I think I would focus most of my reflection toward understanding my use of rhetorical knowledge and the processes I use to prepare myself to write and while writing.

 

  1. The category that makes the most sense for me that reflective writing falls into from the WPA outcomes is of critical thinking. The WPA’s definition of critical thinking is ‘Critical thinking is the ability to analyze, synthesize, interpret, and evaluate ideas, information, situations, and texts’. In reflection we analyze our piece, interpret our own ideas and evaluate the processes and conventions. We can then adjust our use of the other outcomes and apply them in future drafts and writing.
